📝 Grant Writing Fundamentals
The 7 Deadly Sins of the Executive Summary (And How to Fix Them) Read More ▼
90% of reviewers decide to keep reading based on the first page. Learn the structural mistakes that scream "amateur" and the framework that screams "fund me."

The Executive Summary is the most valuable real estate in your grant proposal. Yet most organizations treat it as an afterthought—a quick cut-and-paste from the project description. That's a costly mistake.

Sin #1: Starting with Your Organization's History

Funders don't care that you were founded in 1987 by a passionate group of volunteers—at least not yet. Lead with the problem you're solving and the impact you'll make. Hook them first; credential yourself second.

Sin #2: Burying the Dollar Amount

The ask should appear in the first or second paragraph. Make it easy for the reviewer to know exactly what you need and why.

Sin #3: Using Jargon and Acronyms

If your summary reads like alphabet soup, you've already lost. Write for an intelligent generalist, not a specialist in your field.

Sin #4: Forgetting the "So What?" Test

Every sentence should answer: "Why does this matter?" If you're describing a program activity without tying it to an outcome, cut it.

Sin #5: Ignoring the Funder's Language

Mirror the terminology from the RFP or foundation's website. If they talk about "health equity," use that phrase—not "reducing disparities."

Sin #6: Overpromising

Claiming you'll "end homelessness" in 12 months destroys credibility. Be ambitious but grounded.

Sin #7: No Clear Call to Action

The summary should leave the reviewer thinking: "I need to read the rest of this." End with a compelling transition into the full proposal.

🚀 Startup Funding
SBIR vs. STTR: Which Non-Dilutive Funding Path is Right for Your Startup? Read More ▼
A no-nonsense breakdown of the differences, deadlines, and commercialization requirements for tech founders.

If you're a deep tech founder, you've probably heard about SBIR and STTR as sources of non-dilutive funding. But which one fits your startup? Here's the straightforward comparison.

SBIR (Small Business Innovation Research)

Best for: Startups with strong internal R&D capabilities. You can subcontract up to 33% of Phase I work, but the primary research must be done in-house.

STTR (Small Business Technology Transfer)

Best for: Startups collaborating with a research institution (university, federal lab). Requires a formal partnership where the research institution performs at least 30% of the work.

Key Decision Factors

  • Do you have a university partner? If yes, STTR opens doors. If no, SBIR is your path.
  • Technology readiness: Both require proof-of-concept data, but STTR often expects more foundational research involvement.
  • Agency preference: NSF and NIH offer both. DoD heavily favors SBIR. Check the specific solicitation.

Phase I vs Phase II

Phase I is feasibility (typically $50k-$275k over 6-12 months). Phase II is development ($750k-$1.7M over 2 years). You cannot skip Phase I for most agencies.

Commercialization Plan: The Make-or-Break Section

Both programs demand a credible path to market. Your plan must address market size, competition, IP strategy, and revenue model—even at Phase I.

📊 Program Design
Logic Models Don't Have to Be Boring: A Visual Guide for Program Staff Read More ▼
How to create a logic model that actually helps you run your program better, not just one that checks a grant requirement box.

Most program staff groan when they hear "logic model." They picture a dry, academic exercise that exists only to satisfy a grant reviewer. But a well-constructed logic model is a strategic management tool—and it can be engaging.

Start with Outcomes, Not Activities

Reverse-engineer your model. What change do you want to see? Define your long-term impact first, then work backward to identify the outputs and activities that will get you there.

Make It Visual

Ditch the Word table. Use colors, icons, and flow charts. Tools like Miro or Lucidchart turn a static document into a living map your team will actually reference.

Involve the Whole Team

Facilitate a 90-minute session with program staff, finance, and even a board member. When everyone contributes, the model reflects reality—not just the grant writer's assumptions.

Connect to Data

For each output and outcome, note exactly how you'll measure it. This transforms the logic model into an evaluation framework.

Update It Annually

Programs evolve. Your logic model should too. Treat it as a living document reviewed at each strategic planning retreat.

📬 Grant Stewardship
The Post-Submission Protocol: 5 Steps That Turn 'Declined' into 'Funded' Next Time Read More ▼
Most people give up after a rejection. Smart organizations use the feedback to build a relationship. Here's the exact email script to use.

You got the email: "We regret to inform you..." Most people delete it and move on. That's leaving money on the table. Here's how to turn a no into a future yes.

Step 1: Wait 48 Hours (Then Send This Email)

Subject: Thank you – [Grant Name] Application
"Dear [Program Officer Name], Thank you for the opportunity to apply for [Grant Name]. While we're disappointed not to be selected, we remain deeply committed to [mission]. If your schedule permits, we would be grateful for any brief feedback on how we might strengthen a future application. We value the Foundation's perspective and want to ensure we're putting our best foot forward."

Step 2: Actually Listen to the Feedback

If the funder says your budget wasn't competitive, don't argue. Ask clarifying questions: "Would a lower request amount have been more appropriate, or was it the allocation across line items?"

Step 3: Implement and Document Changes

Create a simple spreadsheet tracking funder feedback and the actions you took. When you reapply, reference this explicitly: "Based on your feedback last cycle, we have strengthened our evaluation plan by..."

Step 4: Stay on Their Radar (Gently)

Send a brief update 6 months later: a new program milestone, a relevant publication, or an award you received. Keep it short and non-ask.

Step 5: Reapply Strategically

Don't just resubmit the same proposal. Your new application should feel like a response to a conversation—because it is.

🔎 Prospect Research
How to Read a 990-PF Like a Forensic Accountant Read More ▼
Forget the foundation website. The 990-PF tax return tells you exactly who they fund, how much they give, and who the gatekeeper is.

Private foundations are required to file Form 990-PF annually. This public document is a goldmine of actionable intelligence—if you know where to look.

Section I – Summary: The Quick Snapshot

Look at Line 25: "Contributions, gifts, grants paid." This is the total giving for the year. Compare it to previous years to see if their grantmaking is growing or shrinking.

Part XV – Supplementary Information: The Real Gold

Line 3 lists every grant over $5,000 made during the year. Scan this list for organizations similar to yours in size, geography, or mission. If you see five grants to organizations just like yours, you've found a strong prospect. If you see only grants to Ivy League universities, move on.

Identifying the Gatekeeper

Look at the "Officers, Directors, Trustees" section. Note the names. Often, the program officer isn't listed, but you can search LinkedIn for "[Foundation Name] Program Officer."

Geographic Restrictions

Check the "Limitations on Awards" section. Many foundations state a geographic preference here that isn't on their website.

The Takeaway

Spend 30 minutes with a 990-PF before writing a single word. It will save you dozens of hours applying to foundations that were never going to fund you.
